Planning (Pre-Conference)

In order to best support a wide range of the conference’s audience, some information and materials need to be provided to attendees prior to the conference’s start. The following topics each have corresponding guidelines to ensure the most accessible, engaging experience for everyone in attendance.

Conference Website

Before the conference, it is a good idea to create an accessible conference website with relevant information. The following checklist items include the information a conference website should have, as well as main issues to address.

  • Website must be accessible and adhere to Web Content Accessibility Guidelines (WCAG) 2.1 Level A and AA.
  • Identify the conference software tool being used for the event. If possible, link to any available accessibility documentation for the software.
  • Outline the registration requirements including the conference deadline, membership options, pricing, and accessible alternatives for any inaccessible registration tools or softwares. See the section below on Registration for more information.
  • Identify the following as they relate to the conference:
    • The primary language(s)
    • The primary time zone(s)
    • How breaks will be handled. See the section below on Breaks for more information.
    • Presentation formats (i.e. presentations, panels, videos, etc.)
    • Whether presentations will be live, pre-recorded, or both
    • If presentations will have captioning and transcripts provided
    • If presentations will be accessible after the conference

Conference Software

It is important to be mindful of the accessibility of the conference software used for the event. Consider the following when assessing the product:

Documentation

  • Provide adequate documentation outlining the accessibility features of the conference software.
  • Provide how-to-use documentation to users who use or rely on assistive and diverse technologies.

Captions

  • Have closed captions and real-time transcriptions in the same window as the conference session as opposed to a separate window. This allows for a “running view” of presentations.
  • Allow for captions to be optionally displayed for all sessions.

Sign Language

  • Allow for sign language interpreters to be optionally displayed during all sessions.
  • Identify interpreters to allow them to be easily found. One method of doing this is by putting “Interpreter” in their display name.
  • Allow for the ability to pin interpreters to the screen.
  • If there is more than one interpreter for a session, establish a system for switching views between hand-offs.

Further Support and Help

  • Provide a means for attendees with disabilities to get or request assistance before and during the conference. See the sections below on Pre-Conference Tours and Accessibility Services for more information.

Registration

  • Include the ability to identify additional considerations and needs for a user. This may include:
    • Requesting a sign language interpreter
    • Documentation on the guidances for screen reader access
    • Requesting more assistive services
  • All forms in the registration process need to be accessible. Follow the guidance and requirements for form elements from the WCAG 2.1 which include:
    • Properly identify and label all form inputs
    • Provide error messaging for invalid inputs and guidance on how to fix errors
  • Consider adding a “Code of Conduct” or similar policy that outlines the expected behavior and etiquette for presenters and attendees.

Program Over and Schedule

  • Provide the overall structure of the conference as a program overview, including sessions, breakouts, breaks and intermissions, and social events.
  • Include how to navigate across the conference platform in the program overview. For example, indicate where the user can find the schedule, software documentation, help desks and other useful tools.
  • Provide an accessible conference schedule that includes the following:
    • Date and times with primary time zone
    • Links to access virtual session
    • Presentation artifacts

Sessions and Rooms

When creating the conference, it is important to consider how to set up the sessions and virtual rooms for the events. Take the following into consideration when setting up the sessions using the conference software:

  • Provide an ample amount of information surrounding the session, including the session topic, relevant information and presentation materials.
  • Identify who the session speaker(s) is and if there are any moderators and/or interpreters.
  • Include an alternative description for any forms of media that accompany presentations.
  • If possible, provide a transcription of the session for attendees to access afterward.

Social Hours

Social hours should be incorporated into the virtual conference as a way for participants to relax and converse outside of formal sessions. Consider the following when assessing how to best incorporate accessibility and inclusion in these spaces:

  • Participants should be muted when entering the social room to avoid unnecessary noise.
  • Rooms should be small with moderators who help facilitate the flow of conversation.
  • Avoid announcing when a participant has entered or exited the social hour session. This puts pressure on individuals to engage in conversation when they might want to observe.

Breaks

Breaks need to be incorporated to the virtual conference, as they’re a natural occurrence in conferences. Here are some considerations when deciding when and how to incorporate breaks:

  • Include breaks in the program overview and schedule with return times.
  • Breaks should be 15 to 30 minutes long to allow for participants to take care of their needs outside of regularly scheduled sessions.

Accessibility Services

The following are services that are largely beneficial to providing further support to attendees with regards to accessibility:

Accessibility Chair

An Accessibility Chair is a person or team of people who are familiar with assistive technologies, working with people with diverse needs, and guiding teams to make products more accessible. They have a strong understanding of accessibility considerations, requirements, and needs for the conference platform. Their responsibilities include:

  • Ensuring the accessibility of the conference platform and tools
  • Working with the conference participants to ensure accessible services are included
  • Establishing best practices for preparing and running conference sessions
  • Providing information, guidance, and documentation to conference attendees

Help Desk

The Help Desk is a means for attendees with disabilities to obtain real-time assistance before and during the conference. The Accessibility Chair(s) should work with the conference organizers and teams to organize and designate an effective and accessible platform to host the host desk, either as a tool within the software or an external communication platform. They will also be responsible for monitoring the system before and during the conference. During (Conference)

Sessions

The following are considerations and video accessibility requirements around live and pre-recorded sessions:

  • Allow early access to pre-recorded video assets. Pre-recorded sessions are especially helpful for users to explore and watch beforehand to prepare for the live session. These also allow for people to attend multiple sessions that are happening at the same time, providing more flexibility and freedom to users.
  • Provide a transcript or audio description of content for video-only content. Unless a video is decorative, a transcript of relevant content needs to be supplied to users. Descriptive transcripts are a text version of the speech and non-speech audio information needed to understand the content. They also include text description of the visual information needed to understand the content. Users who are deaf and blind benefit from effective transcripts.
  • Synchronized captions are provided for videos. Captions allow a person who cannot hear the audio of a video to still understand its content. Captions should align with the timing of the audio as it happens in the video.
  • Participants should be muted when joining sessions with a policy for how, when, and who can unmute their microphone during the session. In relation to this, the state of the user’s microphone (muted/unmuted) needs to be clearly understood by the user.
  • Presenters and interpreters should be the only ones with their cameras on during formal sessions and presentations.

Moderators

Moderators are a vital component to creating an accessible experience for all. Their responsibilities include:

  • Announcing how to find and view interpreters, captions, and real-time transcripts
  • Announcing how the chat and/or Q&A features work
  • Coordinating sign language interpreters by ensuring that interpreter and captioning information is displayed on screen or in an identifiable website
  • Monitoring and facilitating the Q&A at the end of the session or presentation
    • Includes reading questions, calling on participants who “raise their hand”, and monitoring the chat log
  • Monitoring and facilitating the Social Hour sessions
  • Specify the moderator by having the “Moderator” title in their name to make them easily identifiable for direct chat messages.

Chat and Questions & Answering (Q&A)

Chat and Q&A features can be fun and engaging, but also highly distracting and intrusive to certain audiences. It is important to be mindful of the pitfalls of certain chat functionality and consider the following when assessing the tool’s accessibility:

  • Provide a separate repository for questions such as within a chat tool like Slack or Discord with appropriate channels.
  • Leverage a Q&A feature for the chat tool, allowing moderators to sift through proposed questions.
  • All questions should be monitored and facilitated through one or several moderators.
  • Log and track issues and problems conveyed in the chat to save for improvements to future conferences.
